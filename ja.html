My UI/UX Guide

  • What would a useful app interface be like?
  • How can we provide a more efficient outcome from our screens?
  • How can we make it so useful that it can become a habit-forming product?

Those are all universal questions that we ask ourselves when thinking about projects involving users.

My latest projects have pushed me to learn about something that we can say is every developer's weak point.

The design part of the whole thing.

By studying it, I realized that in order to be better at it, I needed:

  1. To be more creative while thinking about solutions.
  2. To better understand people's minds to make things more attractive.
  3. To practice following basic design principles to communicate better and more effectively.

While further practicing, I learned concepts that I will keep using in all my future projects, and I'm going to teach you here for free.

Maybe it can be useful for you too, as it was for me. Or maybe you can get only what makes sense for you.

I call it the 3-3-3 principles for a better UX.

The 3-3-3 Principles

Once, I heard that three is some kind of magical number.

It symbolizes simplicity, and everything in a cycle of three can have the meaning of beginning, middle, and end.

These key principles consist of 3 communication goals, 3 basic design rules, and 3 life learnings applicable to UI/UX.

I will try to be clear while explaining each of them.

3 Communication Goals

Once, I read in a book that when a communicator has a message for his audience, he has 3 consecutive goals to achieve in order to deliver it successfully.

The first goal is:

1/3 Cause a reflection

Let's say you are trying to sell something to an audience.

If you start by saying: "This is my product, please buy it", you will instantly ruin your sales because the audience needs more information before buying something from you.

Not only do they need more information, but they also need to feel that buying a product from you is something that will be worth the price they are paying.

And in order to feel that, people need motivation. They need to feel that your product will impact their lives in some positive way.

You need to show them that you know how bad they feel about the problems they have without your product.

You have to lead them to a reflection in their minds.

Make them think: "Wow, this is true, I have those problems. Does this person know how to solve them?"

And then they will give a lot more attention to your message. Then you can possibly make a sale or whatever you are trying to help them achieve.

A reflection can manifest in many shapes, like curious information, life-changing insights, or advice from an authority.

But as salespeople, we can't do much if we only make an audience reflect about the problem they have.

They can say things like: "Okay, it all makes sense, but..." and there are all kinds of imaginable "buts".

And, generally, the sentence after the word "but" is something that kills sales.

You need to be a little bit more precise in your message and achieve the second communication goal, which is:

2/3 Make them take a decision

Okay, you got that making the audience reflect is very good, but it is not enough for you to make a sale. Right?

That happens because, naturally, people's standard reaction to a salesperson is to create objections in those situations.

They need to protect themselves from being scammed, right?

It is our duty to know it and to be prepared when we are communicating.

And sometimes, the objection is just that the audience still has too many choices after our communication.

And many choices simply confuse people's minds.

Being precise with the message can fix that problem.

We have to make it clear to our audience that they don't have many choices or that, by choosing us, they are making the best choice.

So making your audience think:

"Okay, I got that gas-powered cars are not good, you told me that hybrid cars have those other problems too, so I have decided that my next car will be an electric one!"

You caused them a reflection, you gave them choices, and they have taken a decision.

It would be a great outcome if you were an electric car seller, but only that decision should not be enough for you.

You want them to buy it now, right?

But unfortunately, they might say the most feared combination of words after the word "but" ever:

"But not now..."

And then we panic, right?

I'm just kidding. That is why we have the third goal of communication.

3/3 Make them take action

If your audience has taken a decision, that's good. But there is still something that can make it even better.

Their behavior as a consequence of your communication.

And by "behavior", I mean actions like buying a product, subscribing to a list, donating to a cause, cleaning the room, and so on.

If you are an electric car seller, you want your audience to buy from you.

A digital marketing guru from the internet once said:

When people say "Not now", actually they wanted to say "It's not a priority for me".

Which means that they need a really good reason to make some effort to accept your offer.

And the trick here is that you will have to give them some sense of urgency and scarcity.

You can say things like: "The opportunity to pay less is only happening today!"

And it needs to be true, okay?

So people will feel that they only have that short time to benefit from that opportunity.

Then they will become more likely to buy your product.

And, I hope you got it.

The action taken is the third ingredient for good and objective communication.

It's all related to good UX design, and I'm going to connect the concepts throughout this article.

3 Basic Design Principles

I used to think like that:

"What differentiates a good design from a bad one is if it is beautiful or if it hurts our eyes."

And by "beautiful", I meant made by someone with some kind of super artistic talent.

Too naive, right?

What I was missing was that there are some patterns that, if present, can make a design very effective.

Visual people have more sensitivity to catch these patterns, but auditory people like me don't capture them very well unless they are explicit and well explained.

It took me a while to learn, but let me try to explain in my own words.

1/3 It's about evoking emotions

Have you ever asked yourself what makes you buy something?

And I'm not asking about product features. I'm asking about what happens to you when you decide to buy something.

When we feel certain emotions, we tend to make decisions and take actions easily, and without thinking too much.

For example, if you know that that product you've always wanted is going to be out of stock soon, it will make you feel that feeling of urgency and scarcity I told you about earlier.

Then, it will make you decide if you will let this chance go or if you will act and buy it at that moment.

In the design field, the main purpose is to get people's attention and evoke emotions.

A "beautiful design", as I told you earlier, means a piece of design that perfectly expresses its purpose and makes the viewer feel the right emotions.

And designs that "hurt our eyes" are the ones that simply can't precisely show their purpose, and the evoked emotion is bad.

That's why busy images or too many buttons and actions on interfaces are a bad practice.

Because the purpose is to make people click, buy, subscribe, right?

You will see my design from 2011, and you can see what might be considered a bad design.

2/3 The AIA Composition

Compositions are how the elements that compose a piece of design or an interface are placed on a stage.

A good composition means that the elements that compose a design are well placed on the viewport and, as a result, can lead the viewer's attention to an action by evoking the right emotions.

And we've just seen that "busy design" evokes confusion, right? And by busy, I mean too many elements competing for your attention on the stage.

That is why the effective composition is only made of 3 elements. I call them the Attract, Intrigue, and Act elements.

Let's see a good example of it:

  1. You can see a big background that is the grid of covers from available movies and shows (Attract).
  2. A middle-sized element that can be all elements in white color, including the email address input. (Intrigue)
  3. And a few small items in red, like the logo, the sign-in button, and the Get Started button. (Action)

Everything is balanced, great color usage, and there is a 3D perspective that gives a sense of infinite possibilities.

It is clear for our eyes to get what to do when landing on this page.

The three elements are clearly sized, they don't compete with each other, and one puts your focus into another.

You can use the AIA Composition with Fibonacci's Golden Ratio, and the results can be amazing!

It is more powerful if you use it with the next one.

3/3 The Perfect Color Proportion

I can't say that there is a magic formula to get a perfect color palette, because it doesn't exist.

But, recently, I discovered that there is another magic three.

And before I knew it, I didn't know how to dose the quantity of colors on a page.

So I chose color palettes, randomly applied them to my CSS, and the results were always too colorful.

Look at the first site I made, in 2011:

Wow, it brings me many flashbacks... I remember I made all the rounded corners with CSS background images.

It's not THAT BAD, but can you see all the signs of a bad design here?

There are many blocks and colors attracting your focus, and you don't know what to do first. So confusing at first sight, right?

What I needed was just a method to dose the quantity of colors, and what I found more than 10 years later was the 60-30-10 proportion.

It means that a portion of 60% of your design should have one color or a shade of this color, another portion of 30% should have a secondary one, and the remaining 10% should have another one.

And I don't need to mention that the AIA Composition can use this same proportion for their sizes, right?

Have a look at a good 60-30-10 proportion applied.

You first look at the big element on the left, then your eyes slide to the right where the middle and small-sized elements lie.

60% blue, 30% white, and 10% gray. Attract, Intrigue, and call to action.

It's like magic. At least for programmers.

And it's important for you to know that these numbers aren't an absolute rule.

You can and should use more than 3 colors, but you have to distribute them in a clean and clear way in order to be effective.

So find a way to balance the 60-30-10 proportion yourself. It will make your design really better.

3 Life Principles We Can Apply To UI/UX

Communication and design are all related to UI/UX, and the better they are, the better the results will be.

Now, how can we assimilate life principles to it and apply them to UI/UX?

1/3 Everything Constantly Changes

The world keeps changing and progressing while other things will inevitably become obsolete, especially in the field of technology.

In my experience, I think I spent too much time working with PHP, and I missed when JavaScript was arising.

But it's not something I regret. It's just a fact that things kept changing. And they will keep changing.

That is why I believe that user interfaces should be improved every time they aren't working or when they could be better.

And we will never know better ways if we don't make tests, validations, and upgrades frequently.

So only the end users will be really impacted by the differences between the changes we've made, and that's why we should always be listening to them.

We have to keep it in mind. End-user satisfaction will change, new processes will be included, and new roles will always emerge.

2/3 Simplicity is Key

You might be thinking: "You are being too obvious, everybody knows that simplicity in design is some kind of rule."

But let's take this to a deeper level.

Whatever your business is, it must have a simple purpose, and this purpose should be achievable within a few clicks.

Let's look at an example from a famous ride-hailing app that everyone probably knows:

Their purpose is clearly expressed. They want to connect private drivers and passengers to a mutual help system, creating a new kind of decentralized economic system.

And it can be easily achieved just by opening the app on a smartphone and choosing where you want to go.

Within a few clicks, the purpose is achieved.

But you know that in the middle of this entire process, there are a lot of complex things going on.

And just the simple output from a complex background will help both driver and passenger.

Because for end users, only the output is what really matters.

And it's our job to simplify things for them.

3/3 What should we give to our users?

From 2016 until 2018, when I lived in Brazil, it was the time when I invested in courses on personal development.

One of them was a course on Dave Elman's Method of Hypnosis.

And in this field, it is not always possible to help someone who is in a trance state.

I mean, you can help someone get into a trance state by following the protocol, but it isn't always that you will be capable of helping them precisely with their emotional problems.

And when it happens, the instructors taught us to give the subject, or the patient, something they called the hypnotic gift.

It consisted of giving the subject positive suggestions and making them feel good emotions before they emerged from the trance state.

So that at least that session could result in a positive outcome, which is more valuable than if we did nothing.

In the UI/UX universe, I believe that we can do something similar.

Sometimes we will need to make small changes or big ones, or sometimes we will need to do everything from zero.

It doesn't matter what, but those big or small changes, or the system's new design, should be a gift for both the current users and the new ones.

Or, at least, let them know that its intentions are all positive.
